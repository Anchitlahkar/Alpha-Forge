First Fully-Distributed Tripartite Entanglement of Remote Atomic Qubits

Score: 9.0 | Category: Quantum Computing

TLDR: Researchers have achieved the first fully-distributed Greenberger-Horne-Zeilinger (GHZ) state across a three-node quantum network using individual remote atomic qubits connected by photonic interconnects, a crucial step for scalable quantum networks and modular quantum computers.

Facts:

  • The paper reports the first fully-distributed Greenberger-Horne-Zeilinger (GHZ) state of qubits across a three-node quantum network.
  • The entanglement was achieved using single atomic memories (individual atomic qubits) as the platform, connected via photonic interconnects.
  • Previous three-node distributed GHZ states were generated using solid-state qubits or atomic ensembles, not individual atomic qubits in a fully-distributed manner.
  • A bounded fidelity of 0.841(17) ≤ ℱ ≤ 0.881(17) was achieved.
  • The entanglement generation rate was measured at 0.095(5) per second.
  • The experiment measured a clear violation of Mermin's inequality.
  • The detection loophole was closed for the first time in a fully-distributed multipartite entangled state.
  • This technology is essential for modular quantum computers, distributed sensing and metrology, and multi-party secure communication protocols.

Takeaways:

  • This work represents a significant experimental breakthrough in quantum networking, demonstrating the viability of individual atomic qubits for building scalable, distributed quantum systems.
  • The ability to generate high-fidelity, fully-distributed multipartite entanglement with individual atomic qubits is a foundational step towards practical modular quantum computers.
  • Closing the detection loophole in a distributed multipartite entangled state enhances the robustness and trustworthiness of quantum communication and computation protocols.
  • This research paves the way for future advancements in quantum internet infrastructure and distributed quantum sensing applications.
  • Investors and startups in the quantum computing and quantum networking space should note this advancement as it validates a key architectural approach for future quantum technologies.
