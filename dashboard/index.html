Scaling the Horizon, Not the Parameters: Reaching Trillion-Parameter Performance with a 35B Agent

AI Research Signal: 9.0
📅 June 29 🌐 Source: Hugging Face Papers
💡 Why It Matters
This paper introduces Agents-A1, a 35B Mixture-of-Experts agentic model that achieves performance comparable to trillion-parameter models on complex, long-horizon tasks. This breakthrough demonstrates a practical path to developing highly capable AI agents without relying solely on massive parameter scaling, significantly improving efficiency and accessibility for advanced AI applications, particularly in scientific and engineering domains. It challenges the conventional wisdom of 'bigger is always better' for model parameters.
📝 Summary (TLDR)
Researchers developed Agents-A1, a 35B MoE agentic model, that matches trillion-parameter model performance on long-horizon tasks by focusing on scaling agent trajectories and abilities through a three-stage training process, offering a more efficient and accessible approach to advanced AI.
📌 Key Points
  • Agents-A1 is a 35B Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) agentic model developed by Shanghai AI Lab.
  • It achieves 'trillion-parameter-level performance' on long-horizon tasks, outperforming or matching much larger models like Kimi-K2.6 and DeepSeek-V4-pro.
  • The core innovation is 'scaling the agent horizon' by focusing on long-horizon trajectories and heterogeneous agent abilities, rather than simply increasing model parameters.
  • A long-horizon knowledge-action infrastructure was built, connecting external knowledge, actions, observations, and verifier outcomes, generating agentic trajectories averaging 45K tokens.
  • Training involves a three-stage recipe: 1) full-domain supervised fine-tuning, 2) training domain-level teacher models for specialized expertise, and 3) multi-teacher domain-routed on-policy distillation with salient vocabulary alignment for efficient knowledge transfer.
  • Agents-A1 shows strong capabilities in scientific reasoning, research-level coding, ML engineering, and scientific tool use, achieving leading results on benchmarks like SEAL-0, IFBench, HiPhO, FrontierScience-Olympiad, and MolBench-Bind.
  • The work aims to provide a practical open model for autonomous research workflows, tool-integrated scientific problem solving, and next-generation AI-for-Science agents.

  • Explore the 'scaling the horizon' methodology and the three-stage training approach (SFT, teacher models, multi-teacher distillation) for developing more efficient and capable AI agents.
  • Evaluate the cost-benefit implications of using smaller, high-performing agentic models like Agents-A1 compared to deploying extremely large parameter models.
  • Consider contributing to or utilizing the Agents-A1 model as an open resource for developing next-generation AI-for-Science agents and autonomous research workflows.

📝 Summary (TLDR)
LiveEdit is a novel framework that enables real-time, diffusion-based streaming video editing with strong content preservation, achieving 12.66 FPS through a three-stage distillation pipeline and an AR-oriented mask cache, making it suitable for interactive and AR applications.

  • Introduces LiveEdit, a novel streaming video editing framework for causal, frame-by-frame editing.
  • Addresses core issues of stable background/non-edited region preservation and low latency in streaming video editing.
  • Employs a three-stage distillation pipeline to transfer editing capability from a powerful bidirectional model to an efficient unidirectional streaming editor.
  • Incorporates an AR-oriented mask cache to reuse region-related computation, significantly accelerating inference.
  • Achieves state-of-the-art visual quality among streaming baselines while drastically boosting inference speed to 12.66 FPS.
  • Establishes a dedicated benchmark for streaming video editing.
  • The method is suitable for interactive and augmented reality (AR) applications.
  • Accepted by ECCV 2026, indicating high academic recognition.

Overcoming the Subthreshold Noise Floor in Fusion-Based Photonic Quantum Computing

Quantum Computing Signal: 8.0
📅 Tue, 30 Jun 2026 00:00:00 -0400 🌐 Source: arXiv quant-ph
💡 Why It Matters
This research addresses a critical challenge in fusion-based photonic quantum computing, a leading architecture for building fault-tolerant quantum computers. By identifying the 'sub-threshold issue' caused by fusion failure and proposing a solution using quantum emitter spins, it significantly advances the feasibility of practical photonic quantum computation. This has direct implications for the future development and commercialization of quantum hardware.
📝 Summary (TLDR)
Fusion-based photonic quantum computing faces a noise floor from fusion failure that prevents achieving necessary logical error rates; however, using quantum emitter spins can reduce this noise floor by orders of magnitude, making the architecture more viable.
📌 Key Points
  • Fusion-based quantum architectures are a leading approach for photonic quantum computing.
  • The 'sub-threshold regime,' where logical error rates must be low for useful applications, has received insufficient attention.
  • Fusion failure imposes a noise floor on logical error rates, preventing all-linear-optics architectures from reaching required rates at low overhead.
  • Using quantum emitter spins in fusion-based architectures significantly reduces this noise floor by orders of magnitude.
  • The use of quantum emitter spins also achieves this improvement at a lower overhead compared to all-linear-optics approaches.
⚡ Action Items
  • Monitor further research and development in fusion-based quantum computing, particularly those integrating quantum emitter spins.
  • Evaluate the potential impact of this architectural improvement on companies and startups developing photonic quantum computing hardware.
  • Investigate the specific material science and engineering challenges associated with implementing quantum emitter spins in photonic circuits.

Quantum Fourier Generative Models Trainable at Large Scale

Quantum Computing Signal: 8.0
📅 Tue, 30 Jun 2026 00:00:00 -0400 🌐 Source: arXiv quant-ph
💡 Why It Matters
This work presents a significant advancement in quantum machine learning, demonstrating a scalable and practical approach to training quantum generative models. By enabling classical training at large scales (1000+ qubits simulated) and deploying on real quantum hardware for fast sampling, it paves the way for near-term quantum advantage in generative AI, outperforming classical baselines in preserving complex data structures.
📝 Summary (TLDR)
Researchers developed a new framework for quantum generative models that can be trained classically at large scales (1000+ qubits) and deployed on real quantum hardware for fast, high-quality sampling, outperforming classical and prior quantum methods.
📌 Key Points
  • Proposes a new algorithmic framework for quantum generative models using parallel Fourier feature maps and a forrelation-type quantum circuit.
  • Introduces a novel training strategy using log-likelihood loss with an unbiased Monte Carlo estimator based on Parseval's identity, enabling efficient classical training at large scales (validated at >1000 qubits on a single GPU).
  • Outperforms prior quantum methods (relying on MMD loss) and classical generative models (normalizing flow, diffusion) by avoiding oversmoothing and preserving multi-modal structure.
  • Successfully deployed trained models on superconducting quantum devices, achieving fast sampling times of approximately 300 µs per sample.
  • Validates the 'train-on-classical deploy-on-quantum' paradigm as a viable and practical path for high-quality, scalable quantum machine learning applications.
⚡ Action Items
  • Investigate the potential for integrating this quantum generative model framework into existing classical AI pipelines for specific tasks requiring multi-modal data generation.
  • Explore the scalability limits and hardware requirements for deploying these models on next-generation quantum devices and assess their performance on more complex, higher-dimensional datasets.
